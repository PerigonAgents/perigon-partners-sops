Section 01

Overview & How it works

The Perigon LinkedIn Post Generator produces three LinkedIn posts for Emma — one per post type — from a single Teams recording. Emma records a brief meeting with herself, downloads the .docx transcript, drops it into a SharePoint folder, and the automation handles everything else. No other input is needed. Posts arrive in her inbox within roughly 10 minutes.

Version 2.0 is a standalone pipeline — it no longer depends on the Meeting Note Bot. Emma can trigger it any time she wants fresh content, independently of any other meeting or workflow.

How the AI step works

Version 2.0 uses a single AI step rather than two in sequence. Step 4 reads the raw transcript text directly and generates all three posts in one pass, guided by the Voice DNA document and the Perigon Partners knowledge base. The prompt includes the full post type instructions, writing rules, quality checks, and the voice guide reference — everything the AI needs is built into the prompt itself, rather than split across two steps.

💡

The quality of the posts reflects the quality of the recording. When Emma talks through real client challenges, strategic tensions, or opinions she's been forming, the AI has strong material to work with. When the recording is brief or mostly administrative, the posts will reflect that. The richest recordings produce the best posts.

What Emma receives

An email sent from agents@perigonpartners.co.uk directly to emma@perigonpartners.co.uk. The email body contains three complete LinkedIn posts in plain text — one Thought Leadership, one Trust Building, one CTA — ready for Emma to review and personalise before posting.

ℹ️

Output is plain text, not HTML. Unlike v1, the posts arrive as clean plain text — easier to read, copy, and edit directly. No formatting to strip out before posting to LinkedIn.

Section 02

Step-by-step guide

Step 1 — Record your Teams meeting

Open Microsoft Teams and start a new meeting with yourself. This doesn't require anyone else in the call. The purpose is to record Emma speaking naturally about her current work — client challenges, strategic thinking, wins, observations, things she keeps coming back to. The more specific and honest the recording, the better the posts.

💡

There's no required structure or format. Talk about one client situation, a market observation that's been nagging at you, something the team said that stuck, or a question you keep getting asked. Aim for 5–15 minutes of substance. The AI is better at finding the post than you are at predicting what will land — just talk.

  1. Start recording and enable transcription In the Teams meeting controls, click the three-dot menu (⋯) and select Start recording. Confirm that transcription is also enabled — you need the transcript file, not just the video.Teams will show a notification that the meeting is being recorded and transcribed.
  2. Talk through your content Speak naturally. Cover current client situations (anonymised), market or regulatory observations, team thinking, recurring questions you're answering, or anything forming in your strategic view right now.
  3. Stop recording and end the meeting Click Stop recording before ending the call. Teams will process the recording and make the transcript available in the meeting recap — this usually takes 2–5 minutes.
⚠️

Never use full client names in the recording. The transcript passes through external AI systems. Always refer to clients by sector, size, or situation only — e.g. "a mid-size financial services firm" rather than a name or identifiable description.

Step 2 — Drop the transcript into SharePoint

Once Teams has processed the recording, download the transcript as a .docx file from the meeting recap. Then drag it into the designated SharePoint folder. This single action triggers the entire automation.

Step 3 — Emma receives the posts by email

  1. Wait approximately 10 minutes The full pipeline takes around 8–10 minutes from the moment the file lands in SharePoint to the email arriving in Emma's inbox.
  2. Check emma@perigonpartners.co.uk The three posts are sent directly to Emma's inbox from agents@perigonpartners.co.uk. Subject line will reference the LinkedIn Generator.If the email lands in junk, mark agents@ as a safe sender to prevent this on future runs.
  3. Read all three posts in full Each post covers one type: Thought Leadership, Trust Building, and CTA. They arrive as plain text, making them easy to copy directly into LinkedIn or a notes app.

Step 4 — Emma reviews, personalises, and posts

  1. Read each post and identify which feels truest to the recording The AI anchors posts to specific content from the transcript. If a post feels generic, the relevant section of the recording was probably thin. Use that as a guide for what to include next time.
  2. Personalise before posting Add a current detail, a specific observation, or a line that only Emma would write. This is the most important step — it's what makes the post feel like Emma rather than a generator.
  3. Post or schedule to LinkedIn Recommended cadence: three posts per week, one of each type. Monday (Thought Leadership) · Wednesday (Trust Building) · Friday (CTA).
⚠️

AI output should never be posted to LinkedIn without Emma's review and personalisation. The posts are strong, specific first drafts — the finished post should always feel like it came from Emma.

Section 03

The three post types

The automation generates exactly one post per type on every run. Together across the week they build both reach and relationship with Emma's ideal client audience — CEOs and C-Suite of financial, infrastructure, and services businesses with 100–2,000 employees.

Post Type 1 Thought Leadership

Goal: Reach and awareness. Position Emma as the most relevant expert voice for her ICP. The reader should finish thinking "she understands my world."

Tone: Expert, opinionated, generous with insight. Never hedged or vague.

Examples: "I attended this event — here are my three takeaways"; "New regulation has just been introduced — here's what it means for a 200-person financial services firm"; "How to navigate [challenge] without losing strategic coherence."

What to avoid: Generic observations that could come from anyone. Staccato sentence fragments used as a stylistic substitute for an actual argument. Announcing a point of view rather than earning it.

Post Type 2 Trust Building

Goal: Deepen relationship with the existing audience. Make followers feel they know Emma and how Perigon thinks. The reader should finish thinking "I feel like I know her."

Tone: Personal, honest, specific. Vulnerable where appropriate. Never vague or motivational.

Examples: A client situation (anonymised) that shows how Perigon thinks; a moment of self-doubt or growth; an internal team win described with warmth and honesty.

What to avoid: Posts centred on identity rather than insight — motherhood, womanhood, or work-life balance as the primary frame. Vague reflections that could have been written by anyone.

Post Type 3 CTA

Goal: Move warm audience members toward a next step with Perigon — a tool, diagnostic, conversation, or service. The reader should finish thinking "that's actually useful — I might take her up on that."

Tone: Value-led. Never pushy. Lead with a genuine insight, end with a low-friction invitation.

Examples: "Try our new ESG compliance tool"; "Let me know how Perigon Partners can help you in this situation"; "Behind on AI strategy? We've built a short diagnostic — happy to share it."

What to avoid: "Let's chat" or "reach out to find out more." Corporate language. Any CTA that feels like a brochure rather than a conversation.

Writing rules applied to every post

The AI prompt enforces these rules automatically. Emma should check for them during review:

RuleWhat it means in practice
150–200 words per postLong enough to make a real argument, short enough to hold attention. Posts outside this range should be trimmed or expanded before posting.
No em dashesThe AI is instructed never to use em dashes (—). If any appear, remove them and replace with a full stop, comma, or restructured sentence.
One exclamation mark maximumEarned, not reflexive. If there's no genuine moment of emphasis, the exclamation mark goes.
Contractions throughout"Don't," "isn't," "you'll." These keep the writing human. If a post reads as formal or stiff, contractions are usually the fix.
British EnglishRealise, organisation, centre, behaviour. The AI is instructed to use British spellings throughout.
Plain text outputPosts are delivered as clean plain text with no HTML formatting, making them easy to copy directly into LinkedIn.
Section 04

Troubleshooting

Nothing arrived — no email, no posts
Likely cause

The SharePoint trigger didn't fire, usually because the file wasn't placed in the correct folder or was saved in the wrong format.

Steps to resolve
  • Confirm the file is a .docx — not .vtt, .pdf, or .mp4. The automation only triggers on .docx files.
  • Confirm the file was placed in exactly the right folder: Business Management › Processes › Tools › LinkedIn Ideas Generator › Emma drop transcripts here. Subfolders or the parent folder will not trigger the Zap.
  • Wait 5 minutes and check again — SharePoint polling has a ~2 minute delay.
  • If still nothing, ask Sally to check the Zapier run history for the "Emma LinkedIn Idea Generator" Zap.
The posts arrived but don't sound like Emma — they're generic
Likely cause

The recording contained limited strategic or opinion-led content. If most of the transcript was operational, logistical, or brief, the AI had thin material to work from and will produce thin output.

Steps to resolve
  • This is expected for certain recording types. Not every session will produce equally strong posts — that's normal.
  • For stronger posts next time: include specific client situations (anonymised), contrarian opinions, recurring questions you keep being asked, or market observations that have genuinely struck you.
  • If substantive recordings are consistently producing generic output, the Voice DNA document in the Step 4 knowledge base may need refreshing. Flag to Sally.
The posts reference incorrect details about Perigon, a service, or a client situation
Likely cause

The Perigon Partners knowledge base file in Zapier is out of date, or the transcript contained an ambiguous reference the AI misread.

Steps to resolve
  • Check whether the incorrect detail relates to Perigon's services, team, or positioning — if so, flag to Sally to update the knowledge base file.
  • Do not use full client names or identifying details in recordings. The AI works best with anonymised descriptions: "a financial services firm with around 300 staff" rather than any name.
Fewer than three posts were generated, or one post type is missing
Likely cause

The AI reached an output token limit, or the transcript lacked enough varied content for the AI to differentiate across all three post types.

Steps to resolve
  • Read the email carefully — all three posts may be present but one may be significantly shorter than expected.
  • If a post type is consistently absent across multiple runs, ask Sally to check the output token limit in the Step 4 Zapier configuration.
The email arrived in junk or spam
Likely cause

Outlook occasionally flags automated emails from agents@ as spam, particularly on first use.

Steps to resolve
  • Mark agents@perigonpartners.co.uk as a safe sender in Outlook settings. This prevents it happening on future runs.
  • Check the junk folder as a first step before assuming the automation didn't run.
ℹ️

For issues not covered here, contact Sally Weatherly (Growth & Operations Lead). Include the date of the recording, the filename dropped into SharePoint, and a description of what was and wasn't received.
